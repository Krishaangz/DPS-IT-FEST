Galaxies

Galaxies are vast systems of stars, planets, gas, dust, and dark matter, held together by gravity. Each galaxy is unique in structure, size, and content, contributing to the mesmerizing variety of cosmic landscapes we observe.

Categories of Galaxies

Spiral Galaxies

These galaxies are characterized by their spiral structure and rotating arms, containing young stars and interstellar matter. The Milky Way is a prominent example.

Elliptical Galaxies

Elliptical galaxies are shaped like elongated spheres, with less interstellar matter and fewer young stars, often dominated by older stellar populations.

Irregular Galaxies

Lacking a distinct shape, these galaxies are often chaotic in appearance, possibly due to gravitational interactions or galactic collisions.

Lenticular Galaxies

Lenticular galaxies are a transition type between spiral and elliptical galaxies, with a prominent central bulge but lacking distinct spiral arms.

Ultra-Diffuse Galaxies

These galaxies are faint and have low stellar density, challenging our understanding of galaxy formation and the role of dark matter.

Dwarf Galaxies

Small and dim, dwarf galaxies are often companions to larger galaxies and serve as building blocks in galactic evolution theories.
